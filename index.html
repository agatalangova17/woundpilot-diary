March 16 – 22, 2026 Week 3

Wound Groups, Image Capture & Proposal Sign-Off

Development Milestone

Work Done

Built the wound grouping system, which organises wounds by anatomical region under each patient record. This included the wound group picker, the per-patient wound list, and the navigation between a patient's record and the wounds they have on file. The group abstraction matches how a single patient can present with multiple wounds at the same time and needed to be in place before the wound-level features could be built on top.

Implemented wound image capture, allowing the user to either take a new photo using the camera or pick one from the photo album. Photographs upload to Firebase Storage with the upload paths protected by the security rules established in Week 2. The first version had a noticeable lag between taking a photo and the image appearing back in the app, since full-resolution camera captures were several megabytes and slow to upload and re-fetch. Fixed by JPEG-compressing each image before upload, which brought file sizes down to a few hundred kilobytes and made the round-trip fast enough for the lag to disappear. Added an early version of a healing-trajectory display in SwiftUI; this was originally drafted as a graph but later replaced with the PUSH score plus explanatory text, which gave clinicians more useful information than a line chart alone.

Received official proposal sign-off.

March 23 – 29, 2026 Week 4

Anatomical Body Localization, AR Scaffolding & Advisor Sessions

Development Milestone

Work Done

Built the anatomical body localization flow, which is how the clinician tells the app where on the body a wound sits. The main view, WoundLocationPickerView, uses an interactive body diagram, and the more anatomically dense areas (hands and feet) have their own dedicated detail sheets, so the user can pick specific anatomical sites. The clinical advisor noted that the hand and foot needed this extra granularity in particular, because wound behaviour and treatment differ meaningfully between sites on the same extremity. Wound location matters clinically because it is one of the strongest signals for narrowing down etiology before any other assessment is done.

Started the AR measurement view by setting up ARMeasureView using ARKit and RealityKit. At that point I had to get the camera feed running, configuring the world tracking session, and laying down the architecture that the actual 4-point measurement system would later be added into.

Held a second round of detailed discussions with Dr. Lang in the run-up to the Progress Review.

April 6 – 12, 2026 Week 6

Questionnaire Architecture & T.I.M.E. Framework Research

Development Milestone Research

Work Done

Rebuilt the clinical questionnaire from a flat static form into a conditional branching system. Instead of presenting the full set of more than 50 possible questions on every assessment, the new design uses conditional logic to surface only the questions that are relevant given the patient's profile and the answers already provided, typically narrowing each assessment to between fifteen and twenty-five questions. This matches how clinicians actually narrow a differential diagnosis through targeted questioning rather than running through every possible item.

Split the questionnaire into clean components: QuestionnaireSteps for the individual question definitions, QuestionnaireOptions for the answer choices, and QuestionnaireContext as the central state manager. The context object tracks every answer recorded so far and evaluates the gating conditions for each subsequent step, which is what drives the conditional branching. Keeping the steps, options, and context as separate concerns made it possible to extend the questionnaire later without rewriting the navigation logic.

Continued writing the report in parallel, drafting the Literature Review and Background sections.

April 13 – 19, 2026 Week 7

4-Point AR Measurement System & PUSH Score Implementation

Development Milestone Research

Work Done

The most technically demanding week of the project. Built the 4-point AR measurement system on top of the scaffolding from Week 4. The user taps four points around the wound boundary and the app computes length, width, and area from the resulting quadrilateral. Initial accuracy on flat surfaces was acceptable from the first version, but accuracy on curved anatomical surfaces such as the knee or heel was very poor, with errors of roughly ±10 to 11 cm. After several rounds of iteration the system converged on a multi-sample raycasting approach: each tap fires 25 rays in a small pattern around the touch point, outliers are filtered out, and the remaining samples are combined with a weighted average. This reduced curved-surface error.

Built the PUSH score in PUSH.swift and PUSHTrajectory.swift. PUSH is an evidence-based three-component score combining wound surface area, exudate amount, and tissue type, and it gives a single number that can be tracked across assessments to indicate whether a wound is healing or deteriorating.

Did a UI pass alongside the AR work. Replaced the emoji icons that had been used as placeholders with SF Symbols throughout, and tightened up the profile, contact, and patient-editing views.

April 20 – 26, 2026 Week 8

Clinical Reasoning Engine & Bilingual Support

Development Milestone Research

Work Done

Implemented the clinical reasoning engine in RulesEngine.swift. This is the core diagnostic component that maps the questionnaire's structured answers to a suggested wound etiology. Rule cascade was derived from the clinical literature and from the scoping discussions with Dr. Lang in earlier weeks.

The cascade is intentionally ordered around clinical safety rather than statistical likelihood. Rare but dangerous conditions are checked first so they cannot be missed. The location-based pathway is then used to narrow the differential before specific clinical signs are evaluated, which matches how a specialist actually reasons in practice. Emergency banner styling was added to make safety-critical findings prominent in the report.

Implemented full English and Slovak bilingual support. Built LocalizedStrings.swift covering UI text, medical terminology, clinical descriptions, and the full questionnaire content. Added a language picker so the user can switch the entire app between English and Slovak.

Dressing Engine, Education Hub & Report View Refactoring

Development Milestone

Work Done

Built the dressing recommendation engine in DressingEngine.swift. The engine takes the wound bed state, exudate level, tissue type, infection status, and patient allergies as inputs, and maps them to an appropriate dressing category from the formulary.

Three earlier features were removed after a scoping review concluded that they did not contribute clinical value in the project's setting: a wound record sharing feature, a clinical tips ticker, and an analytics dashboard. May 4 – 10, 2026 Week 10

Security Layer, GDPR Compliance & Compression Safety

Development Milestone Research

Work Done

Implemented the security layer. Added Face ID authentication through LocalAuthentication, with fallback to password when biometrics are unavailable, unenrolled, or denied. Credentials are stored in the iOS Keychain with hardware-backed encryption. A fifteen-minute inactivity auto-logout was added through InactivityManager, and rate limiting was added to the authentication paths. The security functionality was split into dedicated services: BiometricAuthManager, KeychainManager, RateLimiter, and SecureLogger.

Built the account deletion flow in UserDeletion.swift. Firestore has no built-in cascade delete for subcollections, so the implementation traverses the full document hierarchy (patients, wound groups, assessments, and the corresponding photographs in Firebase Storage) and removes everything tied to the deleted account.

This was the week I transitioned from active development to the evaluation and documentation phase. Spent the remaining time on the thesis write-up, the project diary, and the landing page.

May 11 – 17, 2026 Week 11

Clinical Logic Validation: TC1–TC10 Vignette Evaluation with Dr. Lang

Milestone Research Bug Fix

Work Done

Ran the clinical logic validation with Dr. Lang. The evaluation was conducted as a structured per-case protocol using the ten clinical vignettes. The vignettes covered the seven primary etiology categories addressed by the application, together with three additional cases testing clinically distinctive edge presentations.

The evaluation followed a three-stage protocol. For each vignette, Dr. Lang first recorded an independent diagnosis, clinical reasoning, and proposed treatment, before any application output was disclosed for that case (Stage 1). Then the same case data was entered into WoundPilot and recorded the engine's etiology, treatment, and dressing recommendations (Stage 2). Finally, I assigned alignment scores across three dimensions, and Dr. Lang confirmed the final scores (Stage 3).

Across the ten cases, the application achieved seven full agreements and three partial agreements on etiology (7F / 3P = 70 percent full agreement, 100 percent full or partial). The ≥80 percent etiology agreement success criterion defined in the project specification was met when full or partial agreement is considered, with full agreement at 70 percent. Treatment and dressing appropriateness ratings averaged in the clinically acceptable range across cases.
